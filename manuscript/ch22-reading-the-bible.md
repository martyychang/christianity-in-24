# Hour 22: Reading the Bible

Q: How should I read the Bible?
A: Like any great literature: critically, honestly, and on its own terms — not yours.

## Commentary

Hour 2 introduced the Bible as a library — sixty-six books, roughly forty authors, approximately 1,500 years. It covered the basics: read whole books, know the audience, read with the mission in mind. That was foundation. This is the advanced course.

You've now been through twenty-one hours that have used the Bible extensively. You've seen passages quoted, interpreted, reframed, and sometimes challenged. You've watched this book distinguish between what the text says and what the church has claimed it says. If that process has done its job, you're ready for the tools that make independent reading possible.

Because the point was never for you to take this book's word for it. The point was always for you to read the Bible yourself — and to read it well.

## Genre matters

The single most common reading error is treating every part of the Bible as the same kind of writing. It's not. The Bible contains at least seven distinct genres, and each one has its own rules.

**Narrative** (Genesis, Exodus, Acts) tells stories. The characters are real (or presented as real), the events are sequential, and the meaning often emerges from what happens rather than from explicit statements. When David commits adultery with Bathsheba, the text doesn't need to say "this was wrong." The narrative — Nathan's confrontation, the death of the child, the unraveling of David's house — delivers the judgment.

**Law** (Leviticus, Deuteronomy) prescribes rules for a specific community at a specific time. Hour 8 covered the developmental framing: these were rules for a people who needed explicit guidance. Reading them as binding today requires ignoring the entire arc of the biblical story — including Jesus's explicit reinterpretation of the Law through the two commandments (Hour 13).

**Poetry** (Psalms, Song of Solomon, Lamentations) uses metaphor, imagery, and emotional language. When the psalmist writes "the Lord is my shepherd" ([Psalm 23:1][1]), no one believes God is literally herding sheep. But when Revelation describes a beast with seven heads, millions of people read it as a literal prediction. Same genre rules, wildly different application.

**Wisdom literature** (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Job) offers observations about life — not promises. "Train up a child in the way he should go; even when he is old he will not depart from it" ([Proverbs 22:6][2]) is a general observation, not a guarantee. Parents who raised their children faithfully and watched them walk away from the faith have been crushed by reading Proverbs as a contract.

**Prophecy** (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos) speaks to the prophet's own time first. The prophets were not primarily predicting the distant future — they were addressing the failures of their own generation. When Isaiah writes about a suffering servant (Isaiah 53), the original audience would have heard it in relation to Israel's exile. Reading every prophetic passage as a prediction about Jesus requires flattening the text into something it was never designed to be.

**Epistles** (Paul's letters, James, Hebrews) are correspondence — written to specific churches dealing with specific problems. When Paul tells the Corinthians to settle their legal disputes internally ([1 Corinthians 6][3]), he's addressing a congregation that was embarrassing itself in Roman courts. When he tells Timothy to "drink a little wine for your stomach" ([1 Timothy 5:23][4]), he's giving personal health advice. Not every sentence in a letter is a universal theological principle.

**Apocalyptic** (Daniel, Revelation) uses highly symbolic, coded imagery drawn from a tradition the original audience understood and modern readers mostly don't. This genre requires the most care — and gets the least.

## How the Bible was assembled

The books you hold in a Bible today were not always "the Bible." The process of deciding which books belonged — canonization — took centuries.

The Old Testament canon was largely settled by the time of Jesus, though debates about books like Ecclesiastes and Song of Solomon continued among Jewish scholars. The New Testament canon took longer. The four Gospels and Paul's major letters were widely accepted by the late second century. Other books — Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, Revelation — were debated for generations. The Muratorian Fragment (circa 170 AD) lists most of the current New Testament but includes the Apocalypse of Peter, which didn't make the final cut. Athanasius's Easter letter of 367 AD is the earliest document listing exactly the twenty-seven books of the New Testament as we know them.

Books were excluded. The Gospel of Thomas, the Shepherd of Hermas, the Didache — all were read by early Christians, some widely. They didn't make the canon, not because they were "fake" but because the community that assembled the canon judged them less authoritative or less consistent with the received tradition.

This is not a threat to faith. It's honesty about how the Bible came to be. Human beings — guided by community discernment, theological debate, and the accumulated weight of usage — decided which books belonged. That process was messy, political, and took three centuries. The result is a collection that the Christian community has found authoritative for over 1,600 years. Both things are true: the canon was a human process, and the result has proven its worth.

## Translation shapes meaning

Unless you read ancient Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek, you're reading a translation. And translation is interpretation.

The Hebrew word *almah* in Isaiah 7:14 means "young woman." The Greek Septuagint translated it as *parthenos* — "virgin." Matthew quoted the Septuagint version. That single translation choice shaped the doctrine of the virgin birth and centuries of theology. The original Hebrew doesn't require it.

The Greek word *aion* can mean "age," "era," or "eternity" depending on context. When Jesus speaks of *aionios* punishment ([Matthew 25:46][5]), does he mean "eternal" or "age-long"? The translation you choose determines whether hell is permanent or temporary — and translators have made both choices.

The King James Version, published in 1611, used the English available at the time. "Suffer the little children to come unto me" doesn't mean children should suffer — "suffer" meant "allow." But four hundred years of cultural shift have made the KJV's language a source of confusion as often as clarity.

None of this means the Bible is unreliable. It means reading it requires the humility to recognize that every English Bible is one translation team's best judgment about what the original authors meant. Compare translations. Check footnotes. When a passage seems to say something surprising, ask whether the surprise is in the original text or in the translation.

## Revelation: the most misread book

The Book of Revelation was written by a man named John — probably not the apostle — while exiled on the island of Patmos around 95 AD, during the persecution of Christians under Emperor Domitian.

It is apocalyptic literature. That word — *apocalypsis* — means "unveiling" or "revelation." The genre existed before John wrote; Daniel uses similar imagery, and Jewish apocalyptic texts like 1 Enoch were widely known. The genre has specific conventions: visions, symbolic numbers, cosmic battles, coded references to political powers, and a climactic victory of good over evil.

John's audience knew these conventions. When he described a beast with seven heads ([Revelation 13][6]), they recognized the seven hills of Rome. When he assigned the beast the number 666, many scholars believe they understood it as a numerical cipher for Nero Caesar (the Hebrew letters of his name add up to 666). The "whore of Babylon" sitting on many waters was Rome — the empire built on conquered peoples. The plagues, the horsemen, the cosmic upheaval — all were drawn from a symbolic vocabulary the audience shared.

Modern readers, disconnected from that vocabulary, read Revelation as a literal prediction of future events. This has produced the rapture theology, the Left Behind series, elaborate end-times charts, date-setting for the apocalypse, and a strain of Christianity that is more excited about the world ending than about the mission of making it better.

Revelation is not a calendar. It is not a code to be cracked. It is a letter to persecuted Christians, telling them that the empire that is crushing them will not last, that God's justice will prevail, and that faithfulness matters even when — especially when — it costs everything. The imagery is meant to encourage endurance, not to predict helicopter gunships.

The book ends not with destruction but with restoration:

> "He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away."
— [Revelation 21:4 ESV][7]

That's the hope Revelation offers. Not an escape from the world, but the renewal of it. Not rapture, but restoration. A vision of what the mission is building toward — a world where suffering, death, and injustice have been overcome. By whom? By the people who carried the mission faithfully, even under persecution, even at the cost of their lives.

## Judgment imagery

Several passages throughout Scripture use language of judgment and separation. The most vivid is the Sheep and the Goats:

> "When the Son of Man comes in his glory... he will separate people one from another as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats."
— [Matthew 25:31-32 ESV][5]

The sheep — those who are welcomed — are identified not by their theology, not by their church attendance, not by their doctrinal correctness. They are identified by what they did:

> "For I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me drink, I was a stranger and you welcomed me, I was naked and you clothed me, I was sick and you visited me, I was in prison and you came to me."
— [Matthew 25:35-36 ESV][5]

And the criterion that stings: "As you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me" ([Matthew 25:40][5]).

The goats — those who are turned away — are not atheists or pagans or members of the wrong religion. They are people who saw need and did nothing. They had the opportunity to love their neighbor and chose comfort instead.

This parable is the mission in miniature. The judgment is not about what you believed. It is about what you did — specifically, whether you loved the person in front of you who needed love. That's the two commandments from Hour 13 in their starkest form.

Read alongside the wheat and the chaff ([Matthew 3:12][8]), the narrow gate ([Matthew 7:13-14][8]), and the talents ([Matthew 25:14-30][5]), the picture is consistent: the measure is not belief but faithfulness. Not what you said but what you did. Not what you intended but what you chose when the choice was hard.

## Reading as a lifelong practice

You will not master the Bible. No one does. Scholars who have spent their lives studying a single book — just Romans, just Genesis, just Job — will tell you they're still finding things they missed.

That's not discouraging. It's the point. The Bible is not a textbook to be completed. It's a conversation to be entered. You read it at twenty and understand one thing. You read the same passage at forty, after loss and joy and failure and growth, and it says something entirely different — not because the text changed, but because you did.

Read it with tools: a study Bible with historical notes, multiple translations, a commentary when you're stuck. Read it with people: a group that asks hard questions and doesn't settle for easy answers. Read it with honesty: not to confirm what you already believe, but to discover what you've been missing.

And read it knowing that you will get things wrong. That's not failure — it's the process. The Bible has survived 1,600 years of people getting things wrong about it. It will survive your misreadings too. The point is to keep reading, keep questioning, and keep measuring what you find against the mission: love God, love your neighbor. Everything else is commentary.

## Questions to sit with

* Have you ever read a passage of the Bible and assumed it meant something without checking the genre, the audience, or the historical context? What changed when you looked deeper?
* How does knowing that the canon was assembled by a human process affect your relationship with the text — does it weaken it, or does it invite you to engage more honestly?
* If the Sheep and the Goats defines judgment by action rather than belief, how does your life measure up — not by what you believe, but by what you've done for "the least of these"?
